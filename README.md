# Bob Hummer's Think-A-Word

Bob Hummer was an eccentric magician with a gift for creating mathematically 
based magic. Think-A-Word was never published or marketed by Hummer, but it 
was described by Martin Gardner in his Scientific American columns and again 
in his *6th Book of Mathematical Diversions* (1971). The method was clever 
and involved a small hidden device consisting of five dials:

> *"When the first letter of the word is spelled, the magician glances at the 
wheel and notes the letter opposite any card whatever, say the ace of hearts. 
As soon as his back is turned he rotates the second disk of his gimmick until 
this letter touches the ace of hearts. On his second glance at the wheel he 
notes the new letter opposite the ace of hearts. When his back is turned again, 
he adjusts the third disk accordingly. Similarly for the remaining two letters. 
In other words, the performer himself picks a card and uses it to spell four 
letters. He adjusts his dials so that his card and these four letters are in 
line. Then he turns the entire cylinder until he sees a four-letter word. It 
will be the word the spectator spelled. There is, of course, a chance that more 
than one word will turn up, but the odds are heavily against it. If it should 
happen the magician simply makes more than one guess."*

Karl Fulves published the trick again in *The Collected Secrets of Bob Hummer* 
(1980/81). While the method was ingenious, the trick was not particularly 
practical. However, about 20 years ago I thought it could be turned into an 
online interactive experience. Instead of Hummer's gimmick, a computer program 
could compare the resultant rotations of the dial with a dictionary. Any 
combination of letters that matched a word in the dictionary was likely the 
same word the spectator was thinking of — and this also revealed the playing 
card being used as a pointer on the dial.

In 2004, I contacted Jim Bumgardner, who has a number of similar tricks online, 
and presented the idea to him. He succeeded in programming the trick and 
publishing it online as **Mindpower** at [wheelof.com/mindpower](https://wheelof.com/mindpower/). 
It was programmed in Flash and is no longer active.

The current version came about when I thought of using Claude AI to take care 
of the programming. It incorporates other elements and is still a work in 
progress. The dictionary contains around 6,000 words and not every word you 
try will work. Gardner said that finding two matching words in a search would 
be rare — but in testing this app I did come across them. When that happens, 
the app reveals both words and cards.

There are many ways to develop this further. Voice and animation would be a 
natural addition. But for now I'll stop here and leave that to others.

*David Britland*
